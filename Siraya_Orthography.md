# Siraya Orthography

The spelling and orthography of the Siraya language can be divided into four major traditions, reflecting different historical periods, authors, and dialectal variations.

## 1. The 17th-Century Dutch Gospel Orthography

Created by missionaries such as Daniël Gravius to translate the Gospels and the Catechism, this script was heavily influenced by the unstandardized nature of 17th-century Dutch [1].

- **Dutch and Latin Influence:** The missionaries used **double (geminate) consonants** to indicate that the preceding vowel was short [2][3]. Due to their ecclesiastical Latin training, they used **'j'** to represent the glide 'y', and **'c'** was pronounced as 'ts' before front vowels and as 'k' before back vowels [1].
- **Missing Schwa:** The missionaries did not recognize the *schwa* (ə) as a distinct phoneme and had no dedicated letter for it, instead writing it inconsistently as a short 'i', a short 'u', or an apostrophe [2][4].
- **Palatalization:** In this dialect (believed by some scholars to be the Taivoan dialect), the historical velar fricative was often lost in non-final positions. To represent the resulting sound shifts, the orthography used diacritics to show that adjacent vowels were **palatalized**, shifting *a* to *ä* and *u* to *äw* (e.g., *vahäw* for "new", *wäi* for "sun/day") [5].

## 2. The Utrecht Manuscript (UM) Orthography (Mid 17th-Century)

The Utrecht Manuscript (circa 1635–1636) is an anonymous Dutch-Siraya vocabulary list and dialogue collection [7][8]. It represents a distinctly different dialect from the Gospel texts (likely Siraya Proper) [8][9]. Its orthography and phonological representation contrast sharply with the Gospel texts in three major ways:

- **Consonant Reflexes for 'd':** The UM orthography spells reflexes of Proto-Austronesian *\*d* as **'s'** (e.g., *salom* 'water', *sosoa* 'two'), whereas the Gospel orthography uses **'d'** or **'r'** (*ralum*, *ruruha*) [6].
- **Preservation of the Velar Fricative without Palatalization:** The UM preserves the historical velar fricative in all positions, spelling it consistently as **'g'** or **'ch'** [4][5]. Because this sound was not lost in this dialect, **it does not palatalize adjacent vowels** (e.g., UM spells *vaxo* 'new' and *waxi* 'sun/day', avoiding the *ä/äw* shifts seen in the Gospel texts) [5].
- **Oblique/Locative Suffix:** The UM spells the common oblique and locative suffix as **'-ang'**, compared to the Gospel's **'-an'** [10].

## 3. The Sinkan Manuscripts (18th–19th Centuries)

After the Dutch were expelled in 1662, the Siraya people continued to use the Latin script to write land contracts [8]. Maintained independently by native speakers, this script evolved away from European conventions:

- **Simplified Alphabet & Removal of Marks:** The Siraya writers almost entirely dropped the letters **'x', 'e', 'f', 'y', and 'z'** [8]. They completely abandoned the use of ligatures, diacritics, apostrophes, and hyphens [8].
- **Format:** These handwritten manuscripts lacked punctuation, case distinctions, and clear word boundaries, and they adopted a Chinese-influenced way of writing numbers rather than using positional Arabic digits [8].

## 4. Modern Revivalist Orthography (21st Century)

In recent decades, linguists and Siraya language activists have standardized the orthography for language revival [1][3].

- **Taiwanese Romanization Influence:** To make the language easier for the modern Siraya community to read, the spelling emulates **Peh-oe-ji (Taiwanese Church Romanization)**, heavily utilizing hyphens to separate syllables [1].
- **Simplification:** The nine vowels represented in the Dutch texts were reduced to **six vowels**, merging long and short vowel distinctions [3]. Consequently, modern orthography completely **dropped geminate (double) consonants** (e.g., *vullum* became *vulum*) [3].
- **Treatment of the Schwa:** Although linguists recommended creating a specific letter for the schwa to resolve 17th-century spelling inconsistencies, revivalists chose to omit it in spelling but indicate it in pronunciation guides [3].
- **Dialect Mixing:** When building the modern lexicon, revivalists have to choose between the UM and Gospel spellings. **They often mix the two dialects**, generally preferring the unpalatalized vowels of the UM but combining them with the 'r' consonants of the Gospel dialect (e.g., blending UM *samach* and Gospel *ramäx* into the modern word *ramag* 'light') [3].
